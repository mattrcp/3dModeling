1100-1050 BCE

A Bronze Ewer (Guang)

A bronze ewer (guang) is created, richly decorated with animal motifs like taotie, dragons, birds, and tigers. This piece reflects the mastery of bronze casting during this period and the importance of animals in the rituals and beliefs of the Shang and Zhou dynasties.

1050-975 BCE

A Square Lidded Ritual Wine Container (Fangyi)

A square lidded ritual wine container (fangyi) is created, also featuring taotie motifs and a long inscription commemorating administrative meetings and ritual ceremonies during the reign of King Zhao, the fourth king of the Zhou Dynasty. This piece highlights the development of Chinese writing and the use of bronze inscriptions to record historical events.

550-577 CE

Creation of the Xiangtangshan Cave Temples

During the Northern Qi dynasty (550-577 C.E.), Buddhist art flourished, as seen in the creation of intricate sculptures like the Cosmic Buddha Vairochana, the Western Paradise of the Buddha Amitabha, and the Gathering of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas, which were likely used for teaching and displayed in cave temples.

Ritual wine ewer (gong) with masks (taotie), dragons, and real animals:

The Lidded ritual ewer (guang), a bronze pouring vessel from the Shang Dynasty (c. 1100–1050 B.C.E.), originates from the Middle Yangzi Valley in China. It features a broad spout, a vertical handle shaped like a bird, and elaborate animal motifs, including a prominent taotie mask on its body. Dragons, reptiles, birds, and snake-men are intricately rendered, while the lid displays ram and buffalo head masks. These motifs, though their meanings remain uncertain, likely held ritualistic and symbolic importance within Shang beliefs and ceremonies.

Ritual wine container (fangyi) with masks(taotie), serpents, and birds:

The Square lidded ritual wine container (fangyi), a bronze vessel from the Early Western Zhou dynasty (c. 1050–975 B.C.E.), is shaped like a house with a roof-like lid. Its patinated surface features a taotie, an animal-like mask characteristic of ancient Chinese bronze work, on each side. Decorative bands with paired birds or serpent-like creatures flank the taotie. The vessel's 187-character inscription, repeated on its lid, recounts administrative meetings and rituals held in the capital during the reign of the fourth Zhou king, showcasing both its artistic and historical significance.

Buddha draped in robes portraying the Realms of Existence:

This is a larger-than-life statue carved from stone during the Northern Qi dynasty (550-577 C.E.) in northern China. Although the head and hands are missing, the remaining sculpture depicts a standing figure wearing a monk's robe. Intricate carvings cover the entire robe, and the front depicts a symbolic map of the Buddhist universe, called the Realms of Existence. Starting from the top, the carvings depict a hierarchy of realms: divinities, Mount Meru (the center of the universe), demigods, humans, animals, spirits, and lastly, sufferers in Buddhist hells. The back of the robe shows a similar hierarchy of heaven above earth, and the sleeves feature stories about the past lives of Shakyamuni Buddha, the historical Buddha, and the concept of merit and rebirth. The detailed and organized carvings are not merely decorative patterns but represent Vairochana Buddha, the Cosmic Buddha. Buddhist texts describe Vairochana as the force behind all phenomena in the Buddhist universe, and these scenes are thought to be spiritual emanations rising from the Buddha. This sculpture may have been created to teach the basic tenets of Buddhism to those who could not read

Western Paradise of the Buddha Amitabha:

The **Western Paradise relief** and the **Cosmic Buddha statue** are both examples of Buddhist art from the Northern Qi dynasty, a period when Buddhism flourished in China with support from the imperial family and various social classes. Both sculptures are large and intricately carved, indicating the sophistication of Buddhist art during this time, likely due to this support. The Western Paradise relief, with its depiction of Amitabha and the Pure Land, aimed to inspire faith in viewers, promising rebirth in a realm of bliss. Similarly, the Cosmic Buddha statue, with its detailed carvings of the Realms of Existence, might have been used in a monastery to visually communicate Buddhist teachings, particularly to those who could not read. Both artworks, originally situated in cave temples, served as powerful visual representations of Buddhist concepts, promoting faith and understanding of Buddhist cosmology and principles.

Gathering of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas:

The Gathering of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas, a large limestone relief from the Northern Qi dynasty, depicts an assembly of Buddhas, bodhisattvas, and blessed souls amidst jeweled trees and lush landscapes. Originally adorned with vibrant colors and gold accents, traces of pigment hint at its former brilliance. Scholars believe this mural once decorated a stupa-pillar within Cave 2 at Southern Xiangtangshan, a renowned Buddhist cave temple complex.
