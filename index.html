Ritual wine ewer (gong) with masks (taotie), dragons, and real animals:

The Lidded ritual ewer (guang), a bronze pouring vessel from the Shang Dynasty (c. 1100–1050 B.C.E.), originates from the Middle Yangzi Valley in China. It features a broad spout, a vertical handle shaped like a bird, and elaborate animal motifs, including a prominent taotie mask on its body. Dragons, reptiles, birds, and snake-men are intricately rendered, while the lid displays ram and buffalo head masks. These motifs, though their meanings remain uncertain, likely held ritualistic and symbolic importance within Shang beliefs and ceremonies.

Ritual wine container (fangyi) with masks(taotie), serpents, and birds:

The Square lidded ritual wine container (fangyi), a bronze vessel from the Early Western Zhou dynasty (c. 1050–975 B.C.E.), is shaped like a house with a roof-like lid. Its patinated surface features a taotie, an animal-like mask characteristic of ancient Chinese bronze work, on each side. Decorative bands with paired birds or serpent-like creatures flank the taotie. The vessel's 187-character inscription, repeated on its lid, recounts administrative meetings and rituals held in the capital during the reign of the fourth Zhou king, showcasing both its artistic and historical significance.

Buddha draped in robes portraying the Realms of Existence:

This stone statue from China’s Northern Qi dynasty (550–577 C.E.) depicts a monk in a carved robe, though its head and hands are missing. On the front, the robe maps the Buddhist universe: divinities at the top, humans, animals, and spirits in the middle, and Buddhist hells at the bottom, with Mount Meru standing central, flanked by dragons and demigods. The back mirrors this cosmic hierarchy, emphasizing the heavens above the earthly realms. The sleeves feature scenes from Shakyamuni Buddha’s past lives, illustrating the principles of rebirth and merit. Despite its incomplete form, the statue embodies the artistry and spiritual vision of the Northern Qi era.

Western Paradise of the Buddha Amitabha:

The Western Paradise, or Pure Land, is Amitabha Buddha’s heavenly realm of peace and beauty, with jewel trees and heavenly music. Faith in Amitabha ensures rebirth in this blissful land. An eleven-foot stone relief from Cave 2 at Xiangtangshan shows Amitabha welcoming souls reborn from lotus blossoms, the earliest known depiction of the Pure Land in Chinese art. Created in the sixth century with royal support, the cave’s sculptures and carvings immersed worshippers in the vision of Amitabha’s divine realm.

Gathering of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas:

The Gathering of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas, a limestone relief from the Northern Qi dynasty (550–577), once adorned Cave 2 at the Southern Xiangtangshan cave temples in Hebei, China. This mural depicts a celestial assembly of Buddhas, bodhisattvas (enlightened beings), and blessed souls, likely set in one of the four directional Buddhist paradises. Surrounded by jeweled trees and lush scenery, the mural’s traces of gold and bright colors hint at its original brilliance. It likely decorated a stupa-pillar, a central feature of the cave. Removed before 1920 due to damage and theft, the mural was acquired by the Freer Gallery of Art between 1921 and 1922, preserving this significant piece of Buddhist art.
